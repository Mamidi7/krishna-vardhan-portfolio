VFS Document Verification

A production OCR pipeline that verifies passports and visa documents across 6 countries. Three OCR engines working in a fallback chain — GCP Vision handles the heavy lifting, EasyOCR catches edge cases, Tesseract is the last resort. The pipeline degraded gracefully when GCP Vision returned low-confidence scores, falling through each engine until it found a confident read. Built for real visa applicants, not a demo.

Building a production OCR pipeline from zero

Building the VFS document verification pipeline taught me a lesson no tutorial could: the fallback chain matters more than the primary engine. GCP Vision handles 80% of documents clean. But when it returns low confidence on a worn Indian passport or a faded Schengen visa, EasyOCR takes over. When EasyOCR fails — usually on low-contrast scans — Tesseract is the last resort with aggressive preprocessing. The real work was designing a scoring system that decides when to fall through without confusing the user. Also learned that users will upload photos of their phone screen showing a passport photo of their actual passport. The system handles that too now.

56 days of GCP AI/ML — what stuck

Fifty-six days of deliberate practice. One GCP AI/ML concept per day, every day, with working code shipped each evening. The biggest surprise: BigQuery ML is genuinely underrated for tabular workflows — you can train a model with SQL. The hardest lesson: evaluating a RAG pipeline is harder than building the RAG itself. Day 37 (custom model serving on Vertex AI with a custom container) broke me for three days straight — the logs were useless, the error messages misleading, and the fix was a single line in the Dockerfile. Day 42 (text-embedding-004 for semantic search on BigQuery) made it all click. Day 56 felt like a graduation.

Data pipelines for caregiving — my father's health stack

The moment that changed how I think about data engineering: watching my father's hemoglobin trend line drop over four days while his clinic results showed a slow decline he hadn't noticed. I built two pipelines that week. One pulls daily step count, HRV, and sleep data from his Amazfit Helio strap through Health Connect. The other watches his Google Drive for new lab PDFs, OCRs them with GCP Vision, and extracts creatinine, hemoglobin, and eGFR into a timeline. Every morning at 7am, a Telegram message shows me the numbers. When the pipeline detected a hemoglobin drop two days before the lab called to flag it, I stopped thinking of this as a project. It's now part of our weekly care routine.

Teaching kids to code with animated HTML worksheets

"Install Python first" is the worst way to start teaching kids to code. So I built animated HTML/SVG worksheets that need nothing but a browser tab. Each worksheet is a visual puzzle: loops become animated spirals that grow as the counter increments, conditionals become branching paths you can see, data structures become cards you drag into place. The secret is the easing curve — cubic-bezier(0.16,1,0.3,1) — which makes every animation feel tactile without being distracting. Six year olds solve loop puzzles. Fourteen year olds build their own. No accounts, no installs, no "first download this." Just a link to open.
